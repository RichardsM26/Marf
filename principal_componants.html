What is Principal Component Analysis?

Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a method of reducing large data sets into fewer variables yet keeping the key data trends. PCA identifies uncorrelated ocmponents which capture the most variance, which simplifies the data and males analysis faster and more efficient.

Why do we need PCA?

We need to visualise data, but we cannot visualise all variables at once. We could pick two and plot them against each other, but we would lose the information from all the other variables that might influence the outcome. PCA solves this by taking all the variables, combines them, and producing new variables which are called principal components (PCs). The first few PCs contains most of the information in the data set, and PCs are ranked from most to least important.

What is dimensionality reduction?

PCA is a linear dimensionality reduction algorithm. This means the data is transformed from data with high-dimensional space (many columns/variables) into a low dimensional space, while preserving as much of the original data as possible. There are three common dimensionality reduction algorithms: PCA, tSNE and UMAP.

How is PC1 formed?

Imagine there is a table where the data rows are participants and the variables are height, weight, etc. If you plot them against each other, each person is one dot on the plot. Height goes on one axis, weight on the other. In this example only two variables are used when in real life the computer puts all the variables in at once from the beginning.

  1. Plot the height and weight across all participants, like a normal linear graph. You simply draw a standard graph with height on the x-axis and weight on the y-axis and place a dot for each person at their exact values. So, Person 1 (170cm, 70kg) gets a dot at position (170, 70). Every participant becomes one dot on this graph. You now have a cloud of dots.

  2. Find the center of the data by calculating the average height across all participants and the average weight. For example, the average height may be 172cm and the average weight might be 75kg. The center of this data is therefore 172, 75 on the graph.

  3. Shift the data so the origin sits at the center point. You move every single dot so that the center point (172, 75) lands exactly on (0, 0) — the origin. You do this by subtracting the average from every participant’s values. So, Person 1 who was at (170, 70) would move to (170-172, 70-75) = (-2, -5). Every dot shift by the same amount. The cloud of dots looks the same shape; it has just moved so it is now centered at (0,0). This step is called mean centering and is important because it means the PC1 line will always pass through the middle of the data.

  4. For each participant’s dot, you draw a line from that dot that hits your PC1 candidate line at a right angle (90 degrees). Where it hits is called the projected point. Think of it as each dot casting a shadow straight down onto the PC1 line. The original dot does not move — you are just finding its corresponding position on the PC1 line. You do this for every single participant. Project each person’s dot onto that line. On the diagram you want the dotted line to

  1. For each participant, measure how far their projected point (their shadow) is from the origin (0,0) along the PC1 line. Some participants will have projected points far from the origin, some will be close. You now have one distance value per participant.
  2. Take each distance from step 4 and square it (multiply it by itself). You square them so that negative distances (on the left side of the origin) do not cancel out positive distances (on the right side). Then add all the squared distances together to get one single number called SS(distances). A larger SS(distances) means this candidate line is capturing more spread/variation in the data.
  3. Rotate your candidate PC1 line slightly to a new angle, and repeat steps 5, 6, and 7 for this new line position. You keep doing this, testing many different angles, each time calculating the SS(distances) for that angle. The angle that produces the largest SS(distances) is your PC1 line. This line captures the most variation in the data — it is the direction along which your participants differ from each other the most. The loading scores (how much height vs weight contribute to PC1) are taken from the slope of this winning line.

How is PC2 formed?

In a 2D example, PC2 is simply the line through the origin that is perpendicular (orthogonal) to PC1 and no further optimisation needed. This is what is meant by PCs being independent of each other. In higher dimensions, PC2 is found by the same rotation/maximisation process as PC1, but with the constraint that it must be perpendicular to PC1.

How to read a PCA plot

Once all PCs are calculated, the data is rotated so PC1 sits on the horizontal x-axis and PC2 on the vertical y-axis. Each sample becomes a single point on this plot. Samples with similar overall profiles cluster together. PC1 captures more variation than PC2, so distances along the x-axis are more meaningful than distances along the y-axis.

What is a Scree plot?

A scree plot shows how much information (variance) each PC captures on the y-axis. Ideally you want just 2 or 3 PCs to capture around 90% of the variance, so you retain most of the information while still being able to visualise it on a simple plot.

What are loadings?

Loadings tell you how much each original variable contributes to each PC. On a loadings plot: * Variables with a positive correlation are grouped together and tend to change in the same direction * Variables with a negative correlation sit on opposite sides of the origin and tend to change in opposite directions * Distance from the origin matters — larger distance means a bigger impact/contribution to that PC
